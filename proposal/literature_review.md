# Human-in-the-Loop Reinforcement Learning as Budgeted, Model-Based Value-of-Information: A Literature Review for FORESIGHT-HIL

## Abstract

Human-in-the-loop reinforcement learning (HIL-RL) has become the dominant recipe for sample-efficient robotic manipulation: a human watches a learning policy and supplies corrective actions, preferences, or labels at the moments where the agent would otherwise fail. The recipe works, but it is *labor-bound* — its central cost is the human time and attention spent deciding *when* to intervene — and almost the entire literature treats that decision as the human's job in a single-robot, single-operator, sequential setting. This review surveys the literature that bears on a different framing: HIL-RL recast as a *budgeted, model-based Value-of-Information (VoI)* problem, in which a learned dynamics/world model is the instrument that proactively decides whether the agent should continue, self-recover, or spend a scarce human query. We organize the field into ten themes spanning interactive manipulation, robot-gated requesting, model-based RL and world models, decision-aware (value-equivalent) model learning, plasticity and sample efficiency, calibration and active querying, world-model/human coupling, shared-autonomy driving, query-budget theory, and reset-free recovery and tooling. We then position FORESIGHT-HIL against the closest competitors, distill the open research gaps, and summarize the proposed Budgeted Interactive MDP (BI-MDP) formulation together with its anticipatory VoI trigger, cross-environment budget allocation, robustness to imperfect humans, and three model-and-training co-design upgrades (decision-aware dynamics, calibrated triggering, and plasticity-preserving learning). The thesis that emerges from the survey is that the field has matured separately on *model-based sample efficiency* and on *human guidance*, and that the largest remaining gains come from bridging them — using the learned model not merely to imagine rollouts for the policy but to decide, under a budget, when human help is worth its cost.

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

Robotic manipulation is the canonical domain in which reinforcement learning meets the cost of real-world data. Contact-rich and dexterous tasks — insertion, assembly, in-hand reorientation — have long, brittle credit-assignment chains and sparse rewards, so pure online RL is sample-hungry and unsafe to run unattended. The most successful response of the past two years is human-in-the-loop RL, exemplified by HIL-SERL, which combines an off-policy learner that mixes offline and online data with on-demand human interventions to reach near-perfect success on real hardware in one to a few hours of training \cite{luo2025hilserl}. The position survey of Retzlaff et al. argues that RL is *fundamentally* human-in-the-loop, identifying distinct phases (development, learning, evaluation, deployment) at which human involvement is unavoidable and stressing the need to reason about *when and why* a human should be in the loop \cite{retzlaff2024hitl}. The practical takeaway from this line of work is unambiguous: human guidance is the cheapest known route to data efficiency and safety in manipulation, and the supporting software stacks now exist to make it reproducible \cite{luo2024serl}.

Yet HIL-RL's defining bottleneck is the human, and specifically the human's *attention budget*. Existing systems achieve their efficiency by assuming a human is continuously watching a single robot and choosing intervention moments by intuition. That assumption is exactly what caps the throughput of the paradigm and is precisely the thing simulation could relax — thousands of environments can run in parallel, but a human cannot watch thousands of screens. This review is built around two complementary legs of the literature that, taken together, suggest a way out. The first leg is *model-based RL*, whose surveys map the design space of learned dynamics models and their integration with planning and value learning \cite{moerland2023mbrl}, and whose more recent treatments emphasize model generalization error and value-aware model usage \cite{luo2022mbrlsurvey}. The second leg is *human-in-the-loop RL*, which studies how to *use* and *trigger* interventions \cite{luo2025hilserl, liu2026pact, retzlaff2024hitl}. FORESIGHT-HIL bridges them with a single idea: treat the learned model as the *instrument that decides human involvement*. Instead of the human deciding when to intervene, the agent rolls its policy forward in an ensemble world model, estimates the expected cost of an impending failure and its epistemic uncertainty, and decides — for each state, and in parallel sim for each of thousands of environments — whether to continue, to self-recover with a model-based controller, or to spend a scarce query on the human. The remainder of this review surveys the literature that makes each piece of that idea both possible and novel.

## 2. Thematic Survey of the Literature

### 2.1 Human-in-the-loop RL for manipulation

The core line FORESIGHT-HIL extends studies *how to use* intervention data and *how to act* when the policy is failing. HIL-SERL is the system backbone: it pairs a sparse-reward success classifier with SpaceMouse human interventions and an RLPD-style learner, achieving near-perfect success on contact-rich tasks in 1–2.5 hours \cite{luo2025hilserl}. PACT recognizes that intervention-containing trajectories are heterogeneous — part autonomous, part corrected — and uses a learned progress model to locate suboptimal segments, applying a counterfactual preference penalty to the Bellman targets on those segments \cite{liu2026pact}. SiLRI handles *suboptimal* interventions explicitly, learning a state-wise Lagrangian trust between a low-entropy human and a high-entropy RL policy, and notably observes that *intervention timing* strongly affects convergence — a finding that directly motivates treating robustness to imperfect humans as a first-class objective \cite{silri2025}. OHP-RL reframes interventions as online preferences gated by state, feeding naturally into the question of which feedback modality to request \cite{ohprl2026}. ROVE post-trains humanoid VLA policies with optimistic value estimation from mixed-quality data and a cross-embodiment critic distilled from human video, a relevant template for tolerating heterogeneous feedback quality \cite{rove2026}. Sirius is the closest "learn-while-deployed" cousin: it performs human-in-the-loop autonomy during deployment, re-weighting behavioral-cloning samples by approximate human trust \cite{liu2023sirius}. The shared limitation across this group is structural: all assume a single robot and a single human who decides intervention timing reactively. None reasons about a *query budget*, and none uses a learned model to anticipate failure before it manifests — these are precisely the openings FORESIGHT-HIL targets.

### 2.2 Robot-gated requesting: when and whom to ask

A parallel literature, largely in interactive imitation learning, asks the robot itself to decide when to request help. DAgger established the no-regret reduction of imitation to online learning by repeatedly querying an expert on the learner's own state distribution \cite{ross2011dagger}; HG-DAgger and EnsembleDAgger added human-gated control transfer and Bayesian safety gating \cite{kelly2019hgdagger, menda2019ensembledagger}; LazyDAgger and IWR reduced context switching and re-weighted intervention samples to stabilize learning \cite{hoque2021lazydagger, mandlekar2021iwr}. Thrifty-DAgger made the budget explicit, gating queries on novelty and risk to spend a fixed intervention allowance well \cite{hoque2021thrifty}. AIM brings this into the robot-gated regime with an adaptive intervention mechanism whose proxy-Q mimics the human's intervention rule, cutting takeover cost by ~40% relative to Thrifty-DAgger \cite{cai2025aim}. MILE explicitly models *when and how* humans intervene and learns from non-intervention timesteps as well \cite{mile2025}. When the question shifts to *whom* to ask, AC-Teach and the active-imitation-from-multiple-teachers line use Bayesian actor-critics and progress-aware querying to exploit ensembles of suboptimal or non-deterministic teachers \cite{kurenkov2019acteach, zhang2020apil}. The key limitation relevant to our problem is that these triggers are *reactive and largely model-free* — they fire on present novelty/risk or on a learned imitation of the human's rule, rather than rolling a dynamics model forward to anticipate failure, and they operate without a budget shared across many simultaneous environments.

### 2.3 Model-based RL foundations and world models

The instrument FORESIGHT-HIL needs — a forward model whose rollouts and uncertainty drive a decision — comes from model-based RL. The surveys frame the design space: dynamics-model learning, planning/learning integration, and implicit MBRL \cite{moerland2023mbrl}, with explicit attention to model generalization error and value-/policy-aware model usage \cite{luo2022mbrlsurvey}. PETS supplies the canonical uncertainty source: probabilistic ensembles with trajectory sampling that separate epistemic from aleatoric uncertainty \cite{chua2018pets}, and MBRL-Lib provides reusable PETS/MBPO components \cite{pineda2021mbrllib}. MBPO answers "when to trust your model" by branching short model rollouts from real data, bounding the lookahead-horizon trade-off any anticipatory trigger inherits \cite{janner2019mbpo}. RLPD is the underlying learner shared with HIL-SERL and PACT, mixing offline and online data 50/50 for efficient online RL \cite{ball2023rlpd}. Hybrid control for combining model-based and model-free RL is conceptually closest: it switches between controllers by model–policy agreement and uncertainty, which FORESIGHT-HIL generalizes from a model–policy gate to a *human–model–policy* gate \cite{pinosky2022hybrid}. For longer-horizon, contact-rich foresight, latent world models are the natural rollout engines: DreamerV3 masters diverse domains with a single configuration \cite{hafner2023dreamerv3}; TD-MPC2 is a decoder-free latent world model with local trajectory optimization that uses one hyperparameter set across 104 tasks and scales with model/data size \cite{hansen2024tdmpc2}; DayDreamer demonstrates Dreamer-style world models training directly on physical robots, including a reset-free quadruped \cite{wu2022daydreamer}. MuZero shows that a model need only predict reward, value, and policy — not pixels — to plan effectively \cite{schrittwieser2020muzero}, and the transformer/diffusion world-model family (IRIS, TWM, STORM, DIAMOND) demonstrates sample-efficient pixel-domain imagination \cite{micheli2023iris, robine2023twm, zhang2023storm, alonso2024diamond}. The limitation for our purposes is that none of these models was built to *gate a human query*: they optimize next-state or return prediction for the policy, not the decision of whether human help is worth its cost.

### 2.4 Decision-aware and value-equivalent model learning

A model that drives a *decision* should be accurate where the decision is sensitive, not uniformly. The value-aware model-learning literature formalizes exactly this. VAML and its iterative variant IterVAML define a model loss that respects the value function and come with finite-sample error bounds \cite{farahmand2017vaml, farahmand2018itervaml}. The Value-Equivalence Principle makes the idea sharp: two models are equivalent if they induce the same Bellman updates, and the equivalence class shrinks as the set of policies/value functions grows — justifying fitting a model only where it changes the update \cite{grimm2020valueequiv}. The Wasserstein↔VAML equivalence connects the value-aware loss to a tractable metric \cite{asadi2018wasserstein}, model-advantage VAML shows value-aware objectives are deployable in continuous control \cite{modhe2021modeladvantage}, and λ-models demonstrate empirically that decision-aware RL benefits from latent models while cautioning that MuZero-style losses are biased under stochastic dynamics — a caution FORESIGHT-HIL heeds by retaining an MLE anchor \cite{voelcker2023lambda}. MuZero again serves as the canonical value-equivalent model that motivates this objective \cite{schrittwieser2020muzero}. The gap this theme leaves open is concrete: decision-aware models exist, but none has been used to power a *human-query VoI trigger*, where "the decision" is continue/recover/query rather than the policy's value update.

### 2.5 Plasticity, sample efficiency, and offline-to-online

HIL injects *bursts* of off-distribution correction data into a replay stream and rewards high update-to-data (UTD) ratios for sample efficiency — exactly the regime known to damage a network's ability to keep learning. The primacy bias shows that early or skewed data can permanently cap later learning in deep RL, with periodic resets as a remedy \cite{nikishin2022primacy}. ReDo identifies the dormant-neuron phenomenon and recycles inactive units to preserve expressivity with minimal disruption \cite{sokar2023redo}. BBF scales sample-efficient Atari RL via resets plus higher replay ratios, demonstrating that plasticity care unlocks high-UTD training \cite{schwarzer2023bbf}. Mechanistic studies tie plasticity loss to loss-landscape curvature and offer parameterization/optimizer fixes \cite{lyle2023plasticity}, and continual-backprop methods reinitialize least-used units to maintain plasticity indefinitely \cite{dohare2024plasticity}. REDQ shows model-free learning can run at UTD ≫ 1 safely via Q-ensembles and in-target minimization \cite{chen2021redq}, and Cal-QL provides a calibrated value initialization for fast offline-to-online fine-tuning \cite{nakamoto2023calql}. The limitation, and the opening, is that no HIL-RL paper studies plasticity under *intervention-induced* distribution shift, despite the fact that a more plastic learner should recover from each correction with fewer subsequent queries — coupling training health directly to human cost.

### 2.6 Calibration and active querying

For a single decision threshold to transfer across heterogeneous environments, the trigger's predicted-failure signal must be *calibrated*. Guo et al. show modern networks are systematically miscalibrated and that temperature/Platt scaling, evaluated by reliability diagrams and Expected Calibration Error, largely fixes it \cite{guo2017calibration} — the exact toolkit FORESIGHT-HIL applies to the trigger. Cal-QL provides the calibration framing for value functions in the offline-to-online setting, which we port to the trigger rather than the value function \cite{nakamoto2023calql}. On the active side, deep Bayesian active learning supplies uncertainty-driven acquisition functions (BALD) for label-efficient learning \cite{gal2017activelearning}, and the preference-based RL literature contributes feedback-efficiency machinery: SURF uses semi-supervised learning and data augmentation to cut preference queries \cite{park2022surf}, B-Pref benchmarks preference-based RL while explicitly simulating irrational/imperfect teachers \cite{lee2021bpref}, and the foundational preference-RL works — Christiano et al., PEBBLE, and DPO — establish reward learning from human comparisons and its feedback-efficient and direct-optimization variants \cite{christiano2017preferences, lee2021pebble, rafailov2023dpo}. The gap is that none of this calibration/active machinery has been applied to *the trigger's* uncertainty in a HIL-RL system, where it would make a single VoI threshold transferable across a shared, parallel query budget.

### 2.7 World-model and human / VLA coupling

A small but fast-growing literature couples world models with human correction. Hi-WM lets a human edit rollouts inside an action-conditioned world model, using cache/rewind/branch to manufacture dense recovery data offline \cite{hiwm2026}. VLA-in-the-Loop uses a lightweight composite world model as an on-demand corrector for VLA grasping \cite{vlaintheloop2026}. On the VLA side, WorldVLA unifies an autoregressive action model and world model so that the two mutually improve \cite{cen2025worldvla}, and RynnVLA-002 integrates a world model into a VLA to boost real-robot success by roughly 50% \cite{cen2025rynnvla2}. These works establish that world models can predict action-conditioned futures tightly enough to be useful for correction. Their limitation, relative to our goal, is one of *purpose*: they use the world model as an offline data factory or an event-triggered corrector, not as an online instrument that decides — under a budget — whether to query a real human at all.

### 2.8 Shared autonomy and HIL-RL in driving

Autonomous driving provides mature cross-domain prior art on human-gated control sharing. HACO performs human-AI copilot optimization with human-gated takeover in MetaDrive \cite{li2022haco}; HAIM-DRL casts the human as an AI mentor with reward-free proxy values and minimal intervention \cite{huang2024haim}; SafeHIL-RL builds a Frenet dynamic potential field to assess intervention safety and is designed to be robust to guidance quality \cite{huang2024safehil}; and PE-RLHF combines human feedback with physics knowledge under a trustworthy performance bound that holds even with imperfect feedback \cite{huang2024perlhf}. These systems are valuable references for robustness mechanisms, but their triggers are human-gated or rely on physics/potential-field heuristics rather than model-based VoI — leaving the same anticipatory-triggering gap our work fills, should the project ever pivot to driving.

### 2.9 Query-budget and interactive theory

A theoretical line studies querying under explicit budgets and imperfect feedback. Safe learning under irreversible dynamics via "asking for help" achieves sublinear regret *and* sublinear mentor queries through state-transfer, with a self-sufficiency proof \cite{asking2025help} — a template for a budgeted VoI bound. Regret bounds for RL from multi-source imperfect preferences give guarantees of order \(\tilde O(\sqrt{K/M}+\omega)\) under a per-source imperfection budget \cite{multisource2026}, a theoretical analogue of robustness to imperfect humans. Online exploration for RLHF contributes principled, D-optimal-design query selection with provable guarantees \cite{onlinerlhf2025}. This work is rigorous but abstract: it is owned by theorists and not instantiated in a model-based, parallel-sim manipulation system, which is the empirical territory FORESIGHT-HIL occupies (with an optional light theory leg).

### 2.10 Reset-free recovery and tooling/benchmarks

Finally, the "self-recover" branch of the gate and the experimental substrate draw on autonomous-RL and tooling literatures. Leave-no-Trace learns joint forward and reset policies and uses a reset value function to flag impending irreversible states for uncertainty-aware aborts \cite{eysenbach2018leavenotrace} — a direct template for instantiating a model-predicted recover-vs-query margin. EARL formalizes and benchmarks autonomous RL with minimal resets \cite{sharma2022earl}; reset-free dexterous manipulation learns without human intervention via multi-task structure \cite{gupta2021resetfree}; and "A Walk in the Park" shows model-free RL can learn locomotion in 20 minutes, evidence that lightweight learners suffice in many regimes \cite{smith2022walk}. On tooling, robomimic establishes what matters in offline learning from human demos with reproducible datasets and algorithms \cite{mandlekar2021robomimic}; ManiSkill3 provides GPU-parallel contact-rich manipulation at 30k+ FPS with TD-MPC2/PPO/SAC baselines, a candidate substrate for cross-environment budget allocation \cite{tao2025maniskill3}; and CleanRL, d3rlpy, and LeRobot supply single-file baselines, an offline-RL library, and an end-to-end robot-learning stack respectively \cite{huang2022cleanrl, seno2022d3rlpy, cadene2026lerobot}. The collective gap these tools expose is that none provides a *model-based, budget-aware human-query API across vectorized environments* — itself a defensible system artifact.

## 4. Research Gaps

Synthesizing the gap analysis in the proposal with the deeper design-space map in the innovation scan, the field leaves the following open cells, which we adopt as the targets of FORESIGHT-HIL:

- **G1 — Anticipatory, model-predictive triggering.** Existing triggers are reactive and model-free (UniIntervene's value-stagnation \cite{uniintervene2026}; Thrifty-DAgger's novelty/risk \cite{hoque2021thrifty}; AIM's proxy-Q \cite{cai2025aim}). No system rolls a *dynamics ensemble* forward to predict failure *before* it manifests and formalizes the decision as Value-of-Information.
- **G2 — Budgeted query allocation across massively-parallel environments.** This problem *only exists* in large-scale simulation (MJX, ManiSkill3, Isaac Lab) and is entirely unstudied in HIL-RL \cite{tao2025maniskill3}; the parallelism that makes a human a non-issue for sim throughput becomes the central scientific question of how to spend a fixed budget across thousands of environments.
- **G3 — Robustness to imperfect-human timing and quality as a first-class objective.** SiLRI flags intervention timing as decisive \cite{silri2025} and driving works handle it via potential fields or performance bounds \cite{huang2024safehil, huang2024perlhf}, but no manipulation HIL-RL system *controls and sweeps* a family of imperfect humans as a primary evaluation axis — something simulation uniquely enables, with B-Pref as a precedent for simulating imperfect teachers \cite{lee2021bpref}.
- **G4 — Feedback-modality selection.** Choosing the cheapest informative feedback type per state (corrective action vs. preference vs. success label) is nearly untouched in HIL-RL, despite mature preference-RL and active-learning machinery \cite{ohprl2026, christiano2017preferences, lee2021pebble, gal2017activelearning}.
- **G5 — The "model and learner as black boxes" gap.** Prior HIL-RL work, and the project's own first draft, drives the trigger from an off-the-shelf MLE/PETS ensemble and an off-the-shelf RLPD learner. Three sub-gaps make the *instrument itself* part of the contribution: (i) **decision-aware (value-equivalent) dynamics for the trigger (M1)** — no one has used an IterVAML/value-equivalence model to power a human-query VoI gate \cite{farahmand2018itervaml, grimm2020valueequiv, voelcker2023lambda}; (ii) **calibrated trigger uncertainty for a transferable threshold (M2)** — no HIL work calibrates the *trigger's* predicted-failure probability so one threshold transfers across envs \cite{guo2017calibration, nakamoto2023calql}; and (iii) **plasticity under intervention-induced distribution shift (T1)** — no HIL-RL paper studies primacy bias / loss of plasticity / dormant neurons in the bursty, high-UTD regime that interventions create \cite{nikishin2022primacy, sokar2023redo, schwarzer2023bbf}.

## 5. Proposed Approach: FORESIGHT-HIL

FORESIGHT-HIL responds to these gaps with one formulation and four modules, augmented by three model-and-training co-design upgrades. Each element is tied below to the gap it closes and the prior work it builds on.

**Budgeted Interactive MDP (BI-MDP).** We augment an MDP \( \mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma) \) with a human/oracle operator \( \mathcal{H} \) that, when queried at state \(s\), returns feedback \(f\) of a chosen modality \(m\in\mathcal{F}\) at cost \(c_m\); a per-round query budget \(B\) shared across \(N\) parallel environments; and a learned ensemble dynamics model \(\{\hat P_{\phi_k}\}_{k=1}^{K}\) with value function \(Q_\theta\). The objective is to maximize task return subject to total queries \(\le B\) — equivalently, to trace a Pareto front of success rate versus human cost — while remaining robust to a family of human models \( \mathcal{H}\in\Pi_H \) varying in skill, delay, noise, and intervention bias. This formalizes G1–G3 in one object and generalizes the RLPD-based HIL-SERL/PACT setting \cite{luo2025hilserl, liu2026pact, ball2023rlpd}.

**Module A — Foresighted VoI trigger (when to ask; closes G1).** From \(s_t\), the agent rolls the current policy \(H\) steps in each ensemble member to estimate the predicted failure cost \( \widehat{C}(s_t) \), the epistemic uncertainty \( u(s_t) \) (ensemble disagreement, in the PETS sense \cite{chua2018pets}), and the counterfactual cost if the human corrected, \( \widehat{C}_{\mathcal H}(s_t) \). The Value of Information is
\[ \text{VoI}(s_t)=\big(\widehat{C}(s_t)-\widehat{C}_{\mathcal H}(s_t)\big)\cdot \psi(u(s_t)) - c_m, \]
where \(\psi\) up-weights uncertain states. The 3-way gate queries the human if \( \text{VoI}>\tau \); else self-recovers if a model-based recovery has positive margin; else continues. This generalizes hybrid control's model–policy agreement switch \cite{pinosky2022hybrid} to a human–model–policy gate, with the self-recover branch instantiated à la Leave-no-Trace's reset value function \cite{eysenbach2018leavenotrace}, and the lookahead horizon governed by MBPO's "when to trust your model" trade-off \cite{janner2019mbpo}. Optionally, a latent world model (DreamerV3/TD-MPC2) replaces the MLP ensemble for longer-horizon contact-rich foresight \cite{hafner2023dreamerv3, hansen2024tdmpc2}.

**M1 — Decision-aware dynamics for the trigger (closes G5-i).** Rather than fitting the ensemble purely to next-state likelihood, each member minimizes a mixed loss that adds a value-equivalent term to the MLE anchor:
\[ \mathcal L_k = (1-\beta)\,\lVert \hat s' - s' \rVert^2 \;+\; \beta\,\lVert V(\hat s') - V(s') \rVert^2,\qquad \hat s' = s + \hat P_{\phi_k}(s,a), \]
with \(V(s)=\min_i Q_{\theta_i}(s,\pi(s))\) from the RLPD critic. The MLE anchor is always retained (\(\beta<1\), with \(\beta=0\) recovering the original ensemble exactly) because pure value-aware losses are unstable under stochastic/contact dynamics, as documented for λ-models \cite{voelcker2023lambda}. This concentrates both model error and ensemble disagreement on decision-relevant regions, grounded in VAML/IterVAML and the Value-Equivalence Principle \cite{farahmand2017vaml, farahmand2018itervaml, grimm2020valueequiv, asadi2018wasserstein, modhe2021modeladvantage, schrittwieser2020muzero}. The headline ablation is MLE vs. value-equivalent model driving the *same* gate.

**Module B — Budgeted allocation across N parallel envs (closes G2).** Given per-env \( \text{VoI}_i \) and budget \(B\), the agent selects the top-\(B\) queries (or solves a knapsack/submodular selection accounting for redundancy among similar states). The new scientific object is *trigger calibration*: do the queried states correspond to actually failing trajectories? GPU-parallel substrates (MJX, ManiSkill3) make this measurable at scale \cite{tao2025maniskill3}.

**M2 — Calibrated triggering for a transferable threshold (closes G5-ii).** Comparing \( \text{VoI}_i \) across heterogeneous envs is only meaningful if the predicted-failure probability \(p_\text{fail}(s)\) is calibrated. We log a reliability diagram and ECE for "trigger fires ⇔ true impending failure," and optionally apply temperature/Platt scaling \(p_\text{cal}=\sigma(\text{logit}(p_\text{fail})/T)\) on held-out pairs, following Guo et al.'s toolkit \cite{guo2017calibration} in the calibration spirit of Cal-QL \cite{nakamoto2023calql}, with PETS supplying the raw uncertainty \cite{chua2018pets}. Calibrated probabilities let a single \(\tau\) transfer across all \(N\) envs, so the shared budget is spent on genuinely failure-prone states.

**Module C — Robust and multi-modal feedback (closes G3, G4).** The human is modeled as a noisy/delayed policy, and an uncertainty-aware trust weight (cf. SiLRI and PE-RLHF \cite{silri2025, huang2024perlhf}) prevents corrupted feedback from destabilizing training, evaluated under a *sweep* of human models with B-Pref as precedent \cite{lee2021bpref}. The agent selects the modality \(m\in\{\text{corrective action},\text{preference pair},\text{success label}\}\) maximizing expected information per cost — reusing OHP-RL-style state-dependent preferences \cite{ohprl2026}, PACT-style preference-calibrated credit \cite{liu2026pact}, and active-learning/preference-RL machinery for query selection \cite{gal2017activelearning, christiano2017preferences, lee2021pebble, rafailov2023dpo, park2022surf}. Credit is assigned via a model-based counterfactual advantage (rolling the "no-mistake" branch), a tighter alternative to PACT's single-point directional penalty.

**Module D / T1 — Plasticity-preserving learner under intervention shift (closes G5-iii).** Because the RLPD learner runs at high UTD on a replay stream that receives bursts of off-distribution correction data, it is squarely in the regime that induces primacy bias and loss of plasticity \cite{nikishin2022primacy, sokar2023redo, schwarzer2023bbf, lyle2023plasticity}. We add optional ReDo-style dormant-unit recycling \cite{sokar2023redo} and periodic shrink-and-perturb/partial resets, applied to the actor/critic and/or dynamics head, with REDQ as a reference for safe high-UTD operation \cite{chen2021redq} and continual backprop as a continual-learning lens \cite{dohare2024plasticity}. The hypothesis tying plasticity to the headline objective is that a more plastic learner recovers from each intervention with *fewer* subsequent queries, so plasticity care lowers total human cost.

An optional theory leg combines IterVAML's value-aware model-error bound \cite{farahmand2018itervaml} with budget-query regret \cite{asking2025help, multisource2026, onlinerlhf2025} to relate model quality to wasted queries. All three co-design upgrades (M1, M2, T1) are exposed as default-OFF flags, so the baseline configuration reproduces the current system exactly.

## 6. Objectives and Expected Contributions

The project's objectives and expected contributions follow directly from the gaps and method above:

1. **BI-MDP formulation** of HIL-RL as budgeted, model-based VoI, unifying *when*, *whom/what*, and *how-robustly* to ask in a single object.
2. **The FORESIGHT-HIL algorithm**: a foresighted VoI trigger, budgeted parallel allocation, and robust multi-modal feedback use, built on the RLPD/HIL-SERL backbone \cite{ball2023rlpd, luo2025hilserl}.
3. **The first study of query-budget allocation across massively-parallel simulation** — a new problem and metric (success-vs-human-cost Pareto front, trigger precision/recall and calibration) enabled by GPU-parallel substrates \cite{tao2025maniskill3}.
4. **A reproducible simulated-HIL protocol** with a controllable imperfect-human family (clean oracle; action noise; reaction delay; over-/under-intervention; mixture experts), in the spirit of B-Pref's imperfect-teacher simulation \cite{lee2021bpref}.
5. **Empirical human-cost gains** at equal or better success versus PACT, UniIntervene, and SiLRI \cite{liu2026pact, uniintervene2026, silri2025}, with explicit robustness curves over the human-model family.
6. **Decision-aware (value-equivalent) dynamics for the trigger (M1)** — the first use of an IterVAML/value-equivalence model to drive a human-query VoI gate, with a clean MLE-vs-value-equivalent ablation \cite{farahmand2018itervaml, grimm2020valueequiv}.
7. **Calibrated triggering (M2)** — reliability-diagram/ECE diagnostics and temperature/Platt scaling that make a single VoI threshold transfer across many parallel envs \cite{guo2017calibration, nakamoto2023calql}.
8. **Plasticity-preserving HIL training (T1)** — the first study coupling plasticity loss (resets/ReDo) to intervention-induced distribution shift, with the measured thesis that plasticity care reduces total human queries to a success threshold \cite{nikishin2022primacy, sokar2023redo, schwarzer2023bbf}.

Together, contributions 6–8 elevate the work from "a clever decision policy" to a *model-and-training co-designed* HIL system: the dynamics model is optimized for the decision, its uncertainty is calibrated for transfer, and the learner stays plastic so each human query buys more.

## 7. Conclusion

The literature reviewed here converges on a single observation. Human-in-the-loop RL has made robotic manipulation sample-efficient \cite{luo2025hilserl, liu2026pact, silri2025}, and model-based RL has produced forward models accurate and uncertainty-aware enough to anticipate the near future \cite{chua2018pets, hansen2024tdmpc2, janner2019mbpo}. Yet the two have grown up apart: HIL-RL leaves the *when-to-ask* decision to a human and ignores the budget, while model-based RL uses its models to imagine rollouts for the policy, never to decide whether human help is worth its cost. The robot-gated requesting line \cite{cai2025aim, hoque2021thrifty, uniintervene2026}, the decision-aware modeling line \cite{grimm2020valueequiv, farahmand2018itervaml}, the calibration and plasticity lines \cite{guo2017calibration, nikishin2022primacy, sokar2023redo}, and the query-budget theory \cite{asking2025help, multisource2026} each supply a piece, but no existing system assembles them. FORESIGHT-HIL is positioned precisely in that vacancy: by treating the learned model as the instrument that decides human involvement — anticipating failure, calibrating its own uncertainty, allocating a shared budget across many environments, staying robust to imperfect humans, and keeping its learner plastic — it bridges the two legs of the literature and turns simulation parallelism from a perceived weakness of sim-only HIL-RL into its defining scientific contribution.
